# Methods — the science behind this skill

The reasoning behind the Five Principles in SKILL.md. Read this when deciding *how* to teach or correct, especially for a learner whose progress has stalled — the fix is usually a method change, not more drilling.

## 1. Comprehensible Input (Krashen) — material at i+1

Learners acquire language by understanding input slightly **above** their current level ("i+1"). Research converges on a comprehension threshold of roughly **90–98%**: below that, the input is noise; well above it, there's nothing new to acquire.

**How to apply:**
- Calibrate vocabulary and sentence complexity to the learner's CEFR level (below).
- New words/structures should appear in context the learner can *mostly* already parse.
- For reading/listening practice, pick or generate text where they know ~19 of every 20 words.

## 2. Output Hypothesis (Swain) — pushed production

Comprehension alone is not enough. Learners must be **pushed to produce** language (speak, write) at the edge of their ability. Producing forces them to notice gaps ("I don't know how to say this"), which primes acquisition. Feedback on that output closes the loop.

**How to apply:** every session should have the learner *generating*, not only reading explanations. End lessons with "now you write/say one."

## 3. Corrective Feedback — match the type to the moment

Research finding: **explicit, metalinguistic feedback and prompts generally beat recasts** for grammar and vocabulary acquisition — but recasts are valuable for keeping conversation flowing and for pronunciation.

| Feedback type | What it is | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **Metalinguistic** | Name the rule + why ("singular countable noun needs an article") | Grammar, vocab — accuracy work. **Default for correcting writing.** |
| **Elicitation / prompt** | Nudge them to self-correct ("almost — what's missing before 'ticket'?") | Building durable knowledge — self-correction sticks best |
| **Recast** | Reformulate correctly without flagging ("Oh, you *went* there?") | Conversation/fluency, pronunciation — preserves flow |
| **Explicit correction** | Just give the right form | When time-pressed or the learner is lost |

**Rule of thumb:** accuracy mode → metalinguistic + elicitation (the why-table). Fluency/chat mode → recasts, reviewed afterward. Prefer eliciting self-correction over handing over the answer whenever the learner is close.

## 4. Spaced Retrieval Practice — the retention lever

Re-testing material *after* it has begun to fade strengthens the memory far more than massed/back-to-back review. This is the single biggest evidence-backed lever for long-term retention of vocabulary and grammar patterns.

**How to apply:** the `progress/` log schedules the *next review* for each open pattern and recycled word. Expand the interval each time the item survives: **1 day → 3 days → 1 week → 2 weeks → 1 month.** Open each session by spot-checking what's due.

## 5. Focus on Form (not "forms") — grammar in service of meaning

Drawing attention to a grammatical form **within meaningful communication** beats teaching rules in isolation. Tie every pattern to something the learner actually wants to express, so the rule has a hook to hang on.

## CEFR levels — calibrate everything

| Level | Rough ability | How to pitch |
|---|---|---|
| **A1–A2** | Basic phrases, simple present | Short sentences, high-frequency words, lots of encouragement, heavy scaffolding |
| **B1–B2** | Independent user; handles work/study | Most of this user's range; correct patterns, expand vocab, refine accuracy |
| **C1–C2** | Proficient; near-native | Nuance, register, idiom, collocation. **Correct sparingly** — over-correcting advanced learners is counterproductive and demoralizing |

Estimate level early from the learner's output and store it in `progress/`. Re-estimate as they grow.
