# Common Patterns — recurring errors for English learners

The systematic mistakes to hunt for during diagnosis. Each has the **rule**, a **native-ear cue** (how it *sounds* wrong, which trains intuition faster than the rule), and a **quick fix**. Find the *pattern* behind a learner's slips, not the slip itself.

These vary by the learner's first language (L1). Speakers of article-less languages (Slavic, many South/East Asian languages) struggle most with #1; others struggle more with #4 or #6. Note the learner's pattern set in `progress/`.

## 1. Articles (a / an / the / none) — the #1 pattern

- **Rule:** singular countable noun → needs an article. Use **the** when the listener knows *which specific one*; **a/an** to introduce something new or non-specific; **no article** for general plurals and most uncountables.
- **Native-ear cue:** a lone singular noun ("I bought car") sounds like a missing stair-step.
- **Quick fix:** the *first mention* of a thing is usually **a/an**; once introduced, it becomes **the**. ("I saw *a* dog. *The* dog was friendly.")
- See the full decision flow in `cheat-sheets.md`.

## 2. Verb tense — especially perfect vs. simple past

- **Rule:** present perfect (*have done*) links past to now / unspecified time; simple past (*did*) is a finished moment. "I have fixed it" (it's fixed now) vs "I fixed it yesterday."
- **Native-ear cue:** a specific past time word (*yesterday, last week*) forces simple past — "I have fixed it yesterday" jars.
- **Quick fix:** got a specific past time? → simple past. Talking about result-up-to-now or "ever/never/yet/already"? → present perfect.

## 3. Dropped "to be" / auxiliary verbs

- **Rule:** English needs the linking/auxiliary verb. "It a big problem" → "It **is** a big problem"; "I working" → "I **am** working."
- **Native-ear cue:** the sentence feels like it stops short, missing a beat.
- **Quick fix:** every statement needs a verb; check that "is/am/are/was/were" hasn't been skipped before a noun or -ing.

## 4. Prepositions (in / on / at / for / to …)

- **Rule:** largely idiomatic — learned as chunks, not derived. "depend **on**", "good **at**", "interested **in**", "arrive **at/in**".
- **Native-ear cue:** hard to hear until the chunk is memorized — this is a *vocabulary* problem disguised as grammar.
- **Quick fix:** learn the whole collocation as one unit (verb+preposition), not the preposition alone. Recycle them with spaced review.

## 5. Subject–verb agreement & third-person -s

- **Rule:** singular subject → verb takes -s in present ("the bus **arrives**"). "He don't" → "He **doesn't**."
- **Native-ear cue:** "the train leave at six" sounds incomplete to a native ear; "leaves" clicks.
- **Quick fix:** he/she/it → add -s. Watch hidden singulars ("each of the tests **runs**").

## 6. Word order & run-on sentences

- **Rule:** English is fairly strict Subject-Verb-Object; adjectives before nouns; one main idea per sentence.
- **Native-ear cue:** long comma-spliced chains feel breathless.
- **Quick fix:** split run-ons at the comma into two sentences, or join with a connector (*because, so, but, which*).

## 7. Countable vs uncountable nouns

- **Rule:** uncountables take no plural/no *a* ("informations", "an advice" are wrong → "information", "a piece of advice"). Common offenders: information, advice, feedback, software, equipment, knowledge.
- **Quick fix:** memorize the frequent uncountables; use "a piece of / some" instead of "a / -s".

## 8. False friends & near-synonyms

- **Rule:** words that look like an L1 word but differ ("actually" ≠ "currently"; "sensible" ≠ "sensitive"), or near-synonyms with different use ("make" vs "do", "say" vs "tell").
- **Quick fix:** log the specific pair in `progress/` and recycle with example sentences.

---

**Using this file:** during diagnosis, scan the learner's output against these. When two+ slips trace to one entry, that's the pattern — name it, teach its rule + cue, then drill (single-pattern set) and schedule a spaced review.
